~ What are examples of materials (eg natural resources) that effectively have zero cost in certain places, because of e.g. cost of transporting them, and unconventional uses of those materials as a result?
⏺ Great question. Here are some notable examples:
  
  **Natural gas flaring** is the classic case. In remote oil fields (North Dakota's Bakken, parts of Nigeria, Siberia), natural gas extracted alongside oil has historically been worth less than the cost of building pipelines. So it's literally burned off. This has spurred unconventional uses: on-site bitcoin mining using flared gas to power generators, small-scale gas-to-liquids conversion, and powering modular data centers at wellheads.
  
  **Volcanic heat and geothermal energy in Iceland** is essentially free in abundance. This led to Iceland becoming a major aluminum smelter despite having zero bauxite — they import the ore and smelt it there because the energy-intensive process is so cheap. It also drives heated sidewalks, greenhouse agriculture (bananas in Iceland!), and data center hosting.
  
  **Sawdust and wood waste** at lumber mills was historically a disposal problem. This led to the creation of particle board, wood pellets for fuel, and animal bedding industries. In some regions mills would pay you to take it away.
  
  **Whey** was a waste product of cheese making — essentially worthless or a disposal cost. This eventually led to the whey protein supplement industry and its use as animal feed.
  
  **CO2 from fermentation** at breweries and ethanol plants is a byproduct that's often vented. Some facilities now capture it for carbonation, greenhouse enrichment, or dry ice production.
  
  **Sand in desert regions** — abundant but often the wrong type for construction. However, researchers are exploring desert sand for solar thermal storage, 3D printing of glass structures (Markus Kayser's Solar Sinter project), and sandstone-like building blocks.
  
  **Sulfur** is produced in enormous quantities as a byproduct of oil and gas desulfurization. There are literal mountains of sulfur in Alberta and the Middle East. This has driven research into sulfur concrete, sulfur-based batteries, and using it as a road-paving material.
  
  **Seawater/salt** in coastal areas — essentially free. This has led to salt-gradient solar ponds for energy, and in places like the Dead Sea, an entire cosmetics and spa industry built around mineral-rich brine.
  
  **Obsidian and volcanic rock** near active volcanic zones — used historically by indigenous peoples for tools and traded over vast distances, but effectively free at the source. Pumice near volcanoes gets used for lightweight aggregate in construction.
  
  The common pattern is: a material is either a waste product or so abundant locally that its price is zero or negative, and this price signal creates unconventional industries that wouldn't make sense anywhere else.
~ Tell me more about heated sidewalks in Iceland?
⏺ Iceland has been piping geothermal hot water under roads and sidewalks in Reykjavik since the 1960s. The system works similarly to radiant floor heating in a house — hot water circulates through pipes embedded beneath the pavement surface, keeping it snow and ice free through the winter.
  
  The main system covers a significant portion of downtown Reykjavik, something like 50,000+ square meters of sidewalks, parking areas, and pedestrian zones. It's practical rather than luxurious — Reykjavik gets icy winters and the city sits on top of abundant geothermal resources, so the calculus is that the marginal cost of pumping hot water under streets is less than the cost of repeated plowing, salting, and dealing with slip-and-fall injuries and accidents.
  
  The hot water typically comes from the same geothermal district heating system that heats about 90% of Icelandic homes. Water comes out of the ground at high temperatures, gets used for various purposes in a cascade (hottest applications first, then progressively cooler ones), and heating sidewalks is one of the lower-temperature applications in that cascade, so it's making use of water that has already done higher-value work.
  
  It's worth noting this isn't totally unique to Iceland — some cities in other countries have experimented with heated sidewalks using electric coils or gas boilers, like Holland, Michigan (which uses waste heat from a power plant). But those systems are much more expensive to operate. Iceland's version is notable precisely because the energy cost is so low that it's a mundane piece of city infrastructure rather than a novelty.
